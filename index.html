Ending Interest on Bank Excess Reserves

This deck explores the structural shift from a regime of "abundant reserves" to one of "active liquidity." We analyze how ending the subsidy on excess reserves could force trillions of dollars into the private economy, creating a distinct set of market winners and losers.

The Warsh Doctrine: A Philosophy Check

Institutional Humility

Warsh believes the Fed has exceeded its mandate, becoming a "general purpose agency" for fixing economic problems. He advocates for a narrower, more focused Fed that stays out of fiscal policy and partisan politics.

Anti-QE & Sound Money

A vocal critic of "infinite liquidity," Warsh views Quantitative Easing (QE) not as a permanent tool, but as an emergency measure that distorts asset prices and widens inequality. He favors positive real interest rates to discipline capital allocation.

Regulatory Pragmatism

While hawkish on money, he is dovish on regulation. He opposes the "Basel III Endgame," arguing that excessive capital requirements stifle lending. He prefers simpler rules that encourage banks to lend to the real economy rather than hoard capital.

The "Plumbing" Focus

Most uniquely, Warsh focuses on the mechanics of money markets. He believes the current system—where the Fed pays banks to hold cash—is fundamentally broken and discourages the very risk-taking required for economic growth.

The "Floor System" Explained

Since 2008, the Fed has operated a "Floor System." They flooded the banking system with cash (QE). To keep interest rates from falling to zero due to this oversupply, the Fed pays banks interest on these reserves. This creates a "floor" below which banks will not lend.

The Warsh Critique

Warsh argues this system has turned commercial banks into public utilities. Instead of doing the hard work of underwriting loans to small businesses or corporations, banks simply park excess cash at the Fed and collect billions in risk-free profit.

  • Disconnects banks from market discipline.
  • Subsidizes large "Money Center" banks over smaller regional banks.
  • Traps liquidity inside the Fed rather than letting it circulate.

The Pivot: Tiered Reserves Proposal

1. The "Shadow Rate Cut"

Ending the rate on excess reserves forces banks to panic-buy assets to escape the 0% trap. This creates massive demand for bonds, driving market rates down without the Fed officially pivoting. This delivers the low borrowing costs President Trump demands, bypassing the "High for Longer" policy rate.

2. Fixing Housing Affordability

Starved of yield at the short end, banks and funds are forced further out the curve into 10-Year Treasuries and Mortgage-Backed Securities (MBS). This buying pressure crushes long-term yields, directly lowering mortgage rates and solving the affordability crisis.

Market Winners & Losers

The Winners

  • Homebuyers Why: The flow of funds into Mortgage-Backed Securities (MBS) drives mortgage rates down, improving affordability.
  • US Treasury Why: Banks are forced buyers of T-Bills. This lowers the government's cost of borrowing significantly.
  • Bitcoin/Gold Why: Increasing velocity of dollars creates fears of debasement, driving demand for hard assets.

The Losers

  • Big Banks Why: Money center banks (JPM, BAC) lose billions in easy NII. They must work harder for lower margins.
  • The Dollar Why: If US short-term yields crash, the "Carry Trade" appeal of the USD diminishes for foreign investors.
  • Cash Savers Why: Money Market Funds will see their yields drop as the underlying T-Bill yields are compressed.

Deep Dive: Currency & Digital Assets

The Dollar: Stealth Devaluation

Warsh is ideologically a "Strong Dollar" advocate, yet this specific mechanism creates a weaker currency. It is a paradox: he talks tough on inflation, but creates "Shadow QE."

The Yield Trap

Foreign investors hold USD for yield. If Warsh's policy forces T-Bill yields down to 2% while inflation remains at 3%, Real Yields become negative. Capital will flee to other currencies or assets to preserve purchasing power.

Bitcoin: The "Perfect Storm"

This environment is arguably more bullish for crypto than standard rate cuts. It combines high liquidity with a loss of faith in centralized banking plumbing.

  • Liquidity Overflow Trillions of dormant dollars are forced into circulation, finding their way to scarce assets.
  • Stablecoin Legitimacy Warsh prefers private stablecoins (USDC) over CBDCs. This integrates crypto into the US financial rail.
